Here is a Powerful Stranger and Thats Cool

May 19, 2017

Cancer update #12

This week marks my 5th round of chemo. My symptoms this week were relatively mild! I didn't have any of the usual stomach cramping, no nausea. I did have the normal feeling of being totally drained of energy. The worst symptom this week was the taste in my mouth. It is awful. I've read of people getting a metallic taste but for me it is nothing like that, it is more of a slimy feeling, and it makes eating food super gross. But all in all it was a decent chemo week.

Danny was my chemo buddy this week! If there is one great thing to come from going into the infusion center and getting gross chemicals pumped into my chest, it is the total feeling of love and support from my friends. I've had so many people reach out to me and say they want to hang out next time I go in for chemo. I really appreciate it. Makes the few hours I am there go by way faster. So it was great this week hanging out with Danny talking sports and stuff. I usually post a little snap story here but this week my chemo brain got the best of me and I forgot to download it. I lost it in the cyber. Thanks for hanging out Danny!

Other than chemo, this week was an especially great one for a couple of reasons. Andy graduated from law school on Saturday, and we had the whole family down in Seattle. I wrote about Andy last week but I'll just reiterate how proud we all are of Andy's awesome accomplishment. There was a lot of good discussion by the speakers about what a vital role lawyers play in defending Americans. I remember watching all the volunteer laywers at the airports recently after the Muslim ban was enacted, and thinking how cool it was that Andy was going to be able to be helping soon as well. Congrats to the bro on graduating law school! Glad we have a lawyer in the family. As Big Ern once said: "I can do anything I want, hire a lawyer, and I'll walk! Finally Joe is above the law!"

This week was also special for me because I had the best baseball experience of my life on Monday. Cristina, through a friend of my mom's, was put in contact with John Stanton, the majority owner of the Mariners. As a surprise to me, she arranged a tour of Safeco before the game on Monday. I only found out a few hours before gametime... I was planning on having a relaxing night before chemo the next day! We were instructed to wait in the Diamond Club lobby, and an "executive" would meet us and escort us onto the field to watch batting practice. It turned out to be two executives escorting us: Mr. Stanton, and Kevin Mather, the President of the Mariners. I can honestly say it was the most surprised I have ever been. Both of them could not have been nicer, warmer, or more welcoming to a gobsmacked 30 year old that felt like a little kid again. They brought us out to the field and we stood right next to the batting cage as all our favorite players took BP. We chatted with Scott Servais, the Mariners manager, and with a bunch of players. When I say I felt like a kid I really mean it (I'm getting excited just typing up this description). We wandered around the dugout taking handfuls of gum and packets of seeds, I played around on the bullpen phone, Mr. Mather regailed us with old stories of childhood heroes: Ichiro, Edgar, Bone, Griffey, and many others. It was surreal. We got thirsty in the locker room and Robinson Cano got us bottles of water. We got to see the entire stadium from the locker rooms to the press box. They made us reservations at the "Hit It Here Cafe" out in right field, and they gave us some amazing tickets right next to the dugout for the game. We didn't take any cell phone pics, but the team photographer took some good ones that I will post once we get them. I have loved baseball and the Mariners since I was a child, and I am so happy to know that they are run by such kind, warm, generous people. It was an amazing evening, and the happiest I have felt in a long time. Thank you so much to the entire Mariners organization, and to Cristina for somehow being able to keep a surprise from me for a couple weeks!

To close out this blog I wanted to say that one thing I have really enjoyed since my diagnosis is talking about life with some of the people at SCCA that are not doctors. They have an amazing staff, and one of my favorite people to talk to is one of the Staff Chaplains, Mike Yonkers. He is close to my age and has a great perspective on things. I am not a religious person, but I met with him at first because I have a lot of religious friends and family who have shown me amazing support. I wanted to be able to be better at receiving the love and support that was coming from a perspective that I wasn't as familiar with, so I met with Mike to talk about it. In our subsequent meetings, we actually usually don't talk religion at all, just about living with cancer and living with more purpose. I guess the lesson for me in this is that sometimes some amazing support can come from a place that I would never have expected. This week we talked about living more in the present, and Mike introduced me to a poem by David Wagoner called Lost.

  • Stand still. The trees ahead and bushes beside you
  • Are not lost. Wherever you are is called Here,
  • And you must treat it as a powerful stranger,
  • Must ask permission to know it and be known.
  • The forest breathes. Listen. It answers,
  • I have made this place around you.
  • If you leave it, you may come back again, saying Here.
  • No two trees are the same to Raven.
  • No two branches are the same to Wren.
  • If what a tree or bush does is lost on you,
  • You are surely lost. Stand still. The forest knows
  • Where you are. You must let it find you.

This poem really speaks to me. I'm going to keep looking for the Here in the forest, or let it find me.

My family and I are off on a road trip next week to Yosemite so hopefully I will have some great pictures to post next Friday. The entire city of Seattle is mourning Chris Cornell, so I'll leave you with my favorite song of his.

Thanks for reading!

Love, Joe
